1. Intensive vs Extensive Properties

The Fundamental Distinction

Extensive properties scale with the size of the system‚Äîdouble the system, double the property. Intensive properties do not‚Äîthey characterize the state independent of system size.

The Doubling Test

Imagine combining two identical systems into one. Which properties double?

  • Extensive: Volume, mass, energy, entropy, charge, momentum
  • Intensive: Temperature, pressure, density, concentration

6. Symmetry and Invariance

Physics is the Same Everywhere

Fundamental laws do not depend on where you are, when you perform the experiment, or how you orient your apparatus. This is invariance under transformations.

Types of Symmetry

Spatial Translation Symmetry

Physics is the same at different locations ‚Üí Momentum conservation

F = ma works in London, Tokyo, on Mars

Temporal Translation Symmetry

Physics is the same at different times ‚Üí Energy conservation

Laws today = laws yesterday = laws 1 billion years ago

Rotational Symmetry

Physics is the same in all directions ‚Üí Angular momentum conservation

No "preferred" direction in empty space

Gauge Symmetry

Electromagnetic potential can be shifted by a constant ‚Üí Charge conservation

V ‚Üí V + constant doesn't change physics

8. Fields vs Particles

Two Descriptions of Reality

Physics uses two complementary pictures: fields (smooth functions in space) and particles (localized objects). Modern physics reveals they are two aspects of the same underlying reality.

The Particle Picture

Particles are discrete, localized objects with definite properties (mass, charge, momentum). They follow trajectories and obey Newton's laws (classical) or Schr√∂dinger's equation (quantum).

Examples

  • Electrons, protons, neutrons (matter particles)
  • Photons (light particles)
  • Planets, projectiles (classical particles)

The Field Picture

Fields are functions that assign a value (scalar, vector, or tensor) to every point in space. They extend throughout space and carry energy and momentum.

Examples

  • Electric field E(r): force per unit charge at each point
  • Magnetic field B(r): influence on moving charges
  • Gravitational field g(r): acceleration at each point
  • Temperature field T(x,y,z): temperature at each location
Field intensity at distance r: I ‚àù 1/r¬≤
(Inverse square law for point sources)

Field ‚Üí Particle Connection

Quantum Field Theory

In the full quantum description, particles are excitations (quanta) of underlying fields:

  • Photon: Quantum of electromagnetic field
  • Electron: Quantum of electron field
  • Graviton (hypothetical): Quantum of gravitational field

Fields are fundamental; particles emerge as localized disturbances in these fields.

When to Use Which Picture

15. Summary: Interconnections

The Unity of Physics

These themes are not independent‚Äîthey interweave throughout physics, reinforcing and explaining each other.

Key Interconnections

1. Linearity ‚Üî Superposition ‚Üî Conservation

Linear systems allow superposition. Superposition enables conservation laws (e.g., momentum adds in collisions because F = ma is linear).

2. Symmetry ‚Üí Conservation (Noether)

Every continuous symmetry implies a conservation law. This connects abstract geometry to tangible physics.

3. Feedback ‚Üí Stability ‚Üî Linearity

Negative feedback creates stability by linearizing around equilibrium. Positive feedback drives systems away from linearity into chaos.

4. Local ‚Üí Global

Local conservation laws (differential form) integrate to give global conservation (total quantities).

5. Quantization ‚Üí Discreteness ‚Üî Integers ‚Ñ§

Quantum mechanics restricts continuous classical variables to discrete sets indexed by integers (n, l, m).

6. Rates of Change ‚Üí Differential Equations

Nearly all physics laws are statements about derivatives. Solving them gives time evolution.

The Meta-Lesson

Physics is not a catalogue of facts but a coherent framework built on a small number of deep principles. When you understand these core themes, seemingly disparate topics‚Äîoptics, thermodynamics, quantum mechanics‚Äîreveal themselves as different manifestations of the same underlying ideas.

Practical Value for IB Physics

  • Problem-solving: Recognize which principle applies (conservation? superposition? dimensionality?)
  • Conceptual understanding: See connections between topics (Why does Lenz's Law resemble Le Chatelier? Both are negative feedback!)
  • Exam success: IB examiners reward insight into underlying principles, not just plug-and-chug

From Memorization to Understanding

Instead of memorizing 100 disconnected formulas, understand 10 core themes and derive what you need. Physics becomes a language you speak, not a script you recite.

Final Thought

These themes cut across all of physics because they reflect the structure of reality itself. They are not arbitrary human inventions but patterns woven into the fabric of nature. When you master them, you're not just learning physics‚Äîyou're learning to think like nature thinks.
