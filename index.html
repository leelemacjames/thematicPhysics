Core Themes Across Physics

Universal Principles and Fundamental Contrasts in IB Physics

Why These Themes Matter

Physics is not merely a collection of isolated topics—gravitation, electricity, thermodynamics, quantum mechanics. Rather, certain fundamental principles and conceptual contrasts appear repeatedly across all domains. Mastering these cross-cutting themes reveals the deep unity of physics and provides powerful tools for problem-solving and understanding.

The Power of Universal Principles

When you recognize that superposition governs everything from adding forces to wave interference, or that feedback mechanisms explain both planetary orbits and climate systems, you gain insight that transcends individual topics. These themes are the skeleton upon which the body of physics is built.

1. Intensive vs Extensive Properties

The Fundamental Distinction

Extensive properties scale with the size of the system—double the system, double the property. Intensive properties do not—they characterize the state independent of system size.

The Doubling Test

Imagine combining two identical systems into one. Which properties double?

  • Extensive: Volume, mass, energy, entropy, charge, momentum
  • Intensive: Temperature, pressure, density, concentration

Physical Examples

Domain Extensive Intensive
Thermodynamics U (internal energy), V (volume), S (entropy) T (temperature), P (pressure), ρ (density)
Mechanics m (mass), p (momentum), KE (kinetic energy) v (velocity), a (acceleration)
Electricity Q (charge), I (current) V (voltage), E (field strength)
Gravitation M (mass) g (field strength), Φ (potential)

Microscopic → Macroscopic Bridge

Intensive properties are typically averages over microscopic quantities:

  • Temperature T ∝ ⟨kinetic energy⟩
  • Pressure P ∝ ⟨momentum transfer⟩ per area

Extensive properties are sums:

  • Internal energy U = Σ(KE + PE) over all particles
  • Entropy S = kB ln(Ω) — depends on total number of microstates
First Law of Thermodynamics: ΔU = Q − W
All terms are extensive (scale with system size)

Common Confusion

Density ρ = m/V is intensive (ratio of two extensive quantities). Specific heat capacity c is intensive (heat capacity per unit mass). Always check: does it change when you double the system?

2. Linear vs Nonlinear Systems

The Linearity Test

A system is linear if it satisfies:

  1. Scaling: input × a → output × a
  2. Additivity: input₁ + input₂ → output₁ + output₂

If either fails, the system is nonlinear.

Why Linearity Matters

Linear systems are mathematically tractable. We can solve them, predict them, and use superposition to build complex solutions from simple ones. Nearly all fundamental physics (Maxwell's equations, Schrödinger equation, wave equation, Newton's F=ma) is linear in the relevant variables.

Linear Systems

  • Newton's Second Law: F = ma (forces add linearly)
  • Coulomb's Law: Electric fields superpose
  • Wave Equation: Solutions add (interference)
  • Ohm's Law: V = IR (voltage scales linearly)
  • Hooke's Law: F = −kx (small displacements)

Nonlinear Systems

  • Large-angle pendulum: sin(θ) ≠ θ
  • Turbulent flow: Navier-Stokes nonlinearity → chaos
  • Relativistic velocity addition: u ⊕ v ≠ u + v
  • Semiconductor diodes: Exponential I-V curve
  • Material saturation: Magnetic hysteresis

Mathematical Signatures

Property Linear Nonlinear
Equation form y = ax + b y = ax² or y = ex
Superposition Solutions add Solutions don't add
Behavior Predictable, stable Can exhibit chaos, bifurcation
Analytical solution Often exists Rarely exists (numerical methods)

The Small Displacement Approximation

Many systems are linear only for small perturbations. The pendulum equation mθ̈ = −mg sin(θ) is nonlinear, but for small θ, sin(θ) ≈ θ, giving the linear SHM equation θ̈ + (g/L)θ = 0.

Linear: y(x₁ + x₂) = y(x₁) + y(x₂)
Nonlinear: y(x₁ + x₂) ≠ y(x₁) + y(x₂)

3. Feedback and Control

The Universal Dichotomy

Negative feedback: Response opposes the perturbation → stability, equilibrium
Positive feedback: Response reinforces the perturbation → instability, runaway

Negative Feedback: The Restorer

Pattern: Perturbation → Opposition → Return to equilibrium

dx/dt = −k(x − xeq)
Solution: Exponential approach to equilibrium

Examples Across Physics

  • Lenz's Law: Induced EMF opposes flux change (ε = −dΦ/dt)
  • Mechanical damping: Friction opposes motion (F = −bv)
  • Thermistor PTC: Heats up → R increases → I decreases → self-regulating
  • Stefan-Boltzmann radiation: Hot object radiates more → cools → stable temperature
  • Le Chatelier's principle: System opposes imposed changes

Positive Feedback: The Amplifier

Pattern: Perturbation → Reinforcement → Exponential growth/collapse

dx/dt = +kx
Solution: Exponential growth or runaway

Examples Across Physics

  • Ice-albedo feedback: Warming → ice melts → less reflection → more warming
  • Resonance: Driving at natural frequency → amplitude grows
  • Audio feedback: Microphone near speaker → squeal
  • Chain reactions: Each fission triggers more fissions
  • Thermal runaway: NTC thermistor heats → R drops → more current → more heat

Stability Requires Negative Feedback

Domain Negative Feedback (Stable) Positive Feedback (Unstable)
EM Lenz's Law (opposes change) Superconductor flux expulsion
Mechanical Damping (dissipates energy) Resonance (amplifies)
Climate Blackbody radiation (cools) Ice-albedo (accelerates melting)
Electronic Op-amp negative feedback Oscillator circuits

Universal Principle

Every stable system has dominant negative feedback. Positive feedbacks exist but must be weaker or transient. When positive feedback dominates, systems exhibit tipping points, phase transitions, and catastrophic failure.

4. Superposition and Linearity

The Principle of Superposition

When multiple inputs act on a linear system, the total output is the sum of the outputs from each input acting alone.

Why We Can Add Things in Physics

Superposition is not an isolated principle—it's a consequence of linearity. Whenever a system obeys linear equations, superposition follows automatically.

Forces (Newton's Laws)

Fnet = F₁ + F₂ + F₃ + ...
Because F = ma is linear in F

Electric Fields (Coulomb's Law)

Etotal(r) = E₁(r) + E₂(r) + ...
Fields add vectorially at each point

Waves (Wave Equation)

ytotal(x,t) = y₁(x,t) + y₂(x,t)
Constructive/destructive interference

Circuits (Kirchhoff's Laws)

At a node: ΣIin = ΣIout
Currents add algebraically

Standing Waves: Superposition in Action

A powerful example: counter-propagating waves create standing waves through superposition.

y₁ = A sin(kx − ωt) [forward wave]
y₂ = A sin(kx + ωt) [backward wave]

ytotal = y₁ + y₂ = 2A sin(kx) cos(ωt)

Standing wave! Nodes where sin(kx) = 0

Where Superposition Fails

Nonlinear Systems

In nonlinear systems, superposition breaks down:

  • Large-angle pendulum: θ₁ + θ₂ doesn't give period(θ₁) + period(θ₂)
  • Turbulent flow: velocity fields don't add linearly
  • Saturating amplifier: output(input₁ + input₂) ≠ output(input₁) + output(input₂)
Linear System Superposition Valid
Small oscillations (SHM) ✓ Solutions add
Electromagnetic waves ✓ Interference patterns
Quantum wavefunctions ✓ Linear Schrödinger equation
Large-angle pendulum ✗ Nonlinear restoring force

5. Conservation Laws

The Most Powerful Tools in Physics

Conservation laws state that certain quantities remain constant over time. They provide constraints that must be satisfied regardless of the details of the interactions.

The Big Three

1. Conservation of Energy

Etotal = KE + PE + internal energy = constant (isolated system)

Energy can transform between forms but the total never changes. This is the First Law of Thermodynamics: ΔU = Q − W

2. Conservation of Momentum

ptotal = Σmv = constant (no external forces)

In collisions, explosions, or any interaction, total momentum before = total momentum after.

3. Conservation of Angular Momentum

Ltotal = Σmvr = constant (no external torques)

Explains planetary orbits, spinning ice skaters, gyroscopes.

Other Conservation Laws

Quantity Context Key Insight
Charge All interactions Total charge never changes
Mass-energy Relativity E = mc² includes rest mass
Baryon number Particle physics Protons cannot decay (observed)
Lepton number Particle physics Electrons + neutrinos conserved

Noether's Theorem: Symmetry → Conservation

Every conservation law corresponds to a symmetry of nature:

  • Time translation symmetry → Energy conservation
  • Space translation symmetry → Momentum conservation
  • Rotational symmetry → Angular momentum conservation

Conservation laws are not coincidences—they reflect the geometric structure of spacetime.

Why Conservation Laws Are Powerful

You can solve problems without knowing the details of forces or interactions:

  • Collision? Use momentum conservation, no need for contact forces
  • Orbital mechanics? Use angular momentum + energy, no need for F(r) at each instant
  • Chemical reaction? Use charge conservation to balance equations

6. Symmetry and Invariance

Physics is the Same Everywhere

Fundamental laws do not depend on where you are, when you perform the experiment, or how you orient your apparatus. This is invariance under transformations.

Types of Symmetry

Spatial Translation Symmetry

Physics is the same at different locations → Momentum conservation

F = ma works in London, Tokyo, on Mars

Temporal Translation Symmetry

Physics is the same at different times → Energy conservation

Laws today = laws yesterday = laws 1 billion years ago

Rotational Symmetry

Physics is the same in all directions → Angular momentum conservation

No "preferred" direction in empty space

Gauge Symmetry

Electromagnetic potential can be shifted by a constant → Charge conservation

V → V + constant doesn't change physics

Invariance in Special Relativity

The Speed of Light is Invariant

All inertial observers measure c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s regardless of their motion. This seemingly innocuous statement leads to:

  • Time dilation
  • Length contraction
  • E = mc²
  • Spacetime as a unified structure

Broken Symmetries

Not all symmetries hold in nature:

Symmetry Status Consequence
CPT (combined) Exact Fundamental theorem
Parity (P) Broken (weak force) Left-right asymmetry in beta decay
Charge conjugation (C) Broken (weak force) Matter-antimatter asymmetry
Time reversal (T) Broken (weak force) Arrow of time in kaon decay

Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking

Even when laws are symmetric, the ground state may not be. Example: a pencil balanced on its tip has rotational symmetry, but when it falls, it picks a direction—symmetry is "spontaneously broken."

7. Quantization vs Continuity

Discrete vs Continuous

Classical physics assumed all quantities vary continuously (real numbers ℝ). Quantum mechanics revealed that nature at small scales is discrete (integers ℤ).

What is Quantized?

Energy Levels (Atoms)

En = −13.6 eV × Z²/n² (n = 1, 2, 3, ...)
Only discrete values allowed

Angular Momentum

L = nℏ where ℏ = h/2π
Bohr's quantization condition

Charge

Q = ne where e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
Charge comes in integer multiples of e

Photons

E = hν
Light comes in discrete packets

What Remains Continuous?

  • Position: Particles can be anywhere (though probability is quantized)
  • Time: Still a continuous parameter
  • Frequency/Wavelength: Continuous values (but energy = hν is discrete)
  • Temperature: Macroscopic average, effectively continuous

The Quantum-Classical Boundary

Quantization matters when:

Action ~ ℏ or Energy spacing ~ kBT

For macroscopic systems, quantum effects are negligible because ℏ is tiny. The discreteness "washes out" into apparent continuity.

Photons: Bridging Discrete and Continuous

A photon is a wave packet—it has:

  • Continuous frequency ν (wave property, ∈ ℝ)
  • Discrete energy E = hν (particle property, quantized)

This wave-particle duality is central to quantum mechanics.

Classical Quantum
Orbits at any radius Only rn = n²a₀/Z allowed
Continuous energy Discrete energy levels
EM waves with any intensity Photons with E = hν
Predictable trajectories Probability amplitudes

8. Fields vs Particles

Two Descriptions of Reality

Physics uses two complementary pictures: fields (smooth functions in space) and particles (localized objects). Modern physics reveals they are two aspects of the same underlying reality.

The Particle Picture

Particles are discrete, localized objects with definite properties (mass, charge, momentum). They follow trajectories and obey Newton's laws (classical) or Schrödinger's equation (quantum).

Examples

  • Electrons, protons, neutrons (matter particles)
  • Photons (light particles)
  • Planets, projectiles (classical particles)

The Field Picture

Fields are functions that assign a value (scalar, vector, or tensor) to every point in space. They extend throughout space and carry energy and momentum.

Examples

  • Electric field E(r): force per unit charge at each point
  • Magnetic field B(r): influence on moving charges
  • Gravitational field g(r): acceleration at each point
  • Temperature field T(x,y,z): temperature at each location
Field intensity at distance r: I ∝ 1/r²
(Inverse square law for point sources)

Field → Particle Connection

Quantum Field Theory

In the full quantum description, particles are excitations (quanta) of underlying fields:

  • Photon: Quantum of electromagnetic field
  • Electron: Quantum of electron field
  • Graviton (hypothetical): Quantum of gravitational field

Fields are fundamental; particles emerge as localized disturbances in these fields.

When to Use Which Picture

Situation Best Picture Why
Collision of billiard balls Particles Localized, discrete interactions
Light interference Wave/Field Extended, superposition
Photoelectric effect Particles (photons) Discrete energy packets
Electromagnetic induction Field Changing flux induces EMF
Electron in atom Wave/Field Probability amplitude, orbitals

9. Reversibility vs Irreversibility

Time's Arrow

Most fundamental laws are time-reversible: they work equally well forward or backward in time. Yet the world we observe is profoundly irreversible. Why?

Time-Reversible Laws

Newton's Laws

F = ma → If you reverse time (t → −t), v → −v, a → −a
Equation still holds: F = m(−a) with (−v)

Maxwell's Equations

Electromagnetic waves work equally well backward in time (mathematically).

Schrödinger Equation

Quantum evolution is unitary and reversible.

Irreversible Processes

Yet we observe:

  • Hot coffee cools, never spontaneously heats up
  • Broken eggs don't reassemble
  • Ink diffuses in water, never un-mixes
  • Entropy increases: ΔS ≥ 0

The Second Law of Thermodynamics

ΔSuniverse ≥ 0
Entropy never decreases in isolated systems

This is the only fundamental law with a preferred direction of time.

Resolution: Statistical Mechanics

Irreversibility emerges from statistics, not from reversing individual particle motions:

Microscopic vs Macroscopic

  • Microscopic: Individual collisions are reversible
  • Macroscopic: With ~10²³ particles, returning to initial state is staggeringly improbable
Probability of spontaneous decrease in entropy ~ e−ΔS/kB
For macroscopic ΔS, this is effectively zero

Exceptions: Tiny Quantum Systems

For small systems (few particles), fluctuations can temporarily decrease entropy. But averaged over time, entropy still increases.

Domain Reversible Irreversible
Mechanics Elastic collision Inelastic collision (heat generated)
Thermodynamics Ideal reversible process (Carnot) Real processes (friction, diffusion)
EM Oscillating charge (reversible) Radiation to infinity (irreversible)

10. Local vs Global Properties

Where is the Information?

Local: Properties defined at each point in space (fields, densities)
Global: Properties defined for the entire system (total energy, charge)

Local Properties (Fields)

These are functions of position:

  • E(r): Electric field at point r
  • ρ(r): Charge density at point r
  • T(x,y,z): Temperature at position (x,y,z)
  • p(r): Momentum density
Local conservation: ∂ρ/∂t + ∇·J = 0
(Continuity equation)

Global Properties (Integrals)

These are totals over space:

  • Qtotal = ∫ρ(r) dV: Total charge
  • Etotal = ∫ε(r) dV: Total energy
  • Mtotal = ∫ρmass(r) dV: Total mass
Global conservation: dQtotal/dt = 0
(Charge conservation)

Relating Local and Global

Fundamental Connection

Global conservation laws emerge from local conservation laws:

If ∂ρ/∂t + ∇·J = 0 everywhere
Then d/dt ∫ρ dV = 0 (total conserved)

Local laws are more fundamental—they encode causality (no action at a distance).

Causality and Locality

Physics is local: events at point A can only directly affect nearby points. Influences propagate through fields at finite speed (≤ c).

Newton's Gravity (Non-local)

F = Gm₁m₂/r²
Acts instantaneously across space (problematic!)

Einstein's Gravity (Local)

Gravitational field propagates at speed c
Described by curvature of spacetime
Concept Local Form Global Form
Charge conservation ∂ρ/∂t + ∇·J = 0 Qtotal = constant
Energy conservation ∂u/∂t + ∇·S = 0 Etotal = constant
Maxwell's equations ∇×E = −∂B/∂t ∮E·dl = −dΦ/dt

11. Rates of Change (Derivatives)

Physics Describes Change

Nearly every law in physics is a differential equation—a statement about how quantities change. Static descriptions are just special cases where rates happen to be zero.

The Universal Pattern

[Physical Quantity] = d[Something]/dt

Kinematics

v = dx/dt (velocity)
a = dv/dt = d²x/dt² (acceleration)

Dynamics

F = dp/dt (Newton's 2nd Law)
τ = dL/dt (Rotational analogue)

Electromagnetism

I = dQ/dt (current)
ε = −dΦ/dt (Faraday's Law)
V = L(dI/dt) (inductor)

Thermodynamics

P = dE/dt (power)
dS ≥ dQ/T (entropy change)

Why Derivatives?

We observe changes, not absolutes:

  • We don't measure position x; we measure velocity v = dx/dt
  • We don't measure charge Q; we measure current I = dQ/dt
  • Even "static" equilibrium is dynamics with zero rates: dE/dt = 0

Conservation Laws as Zero Derivatives

dp/dt = 0 → momentum conserved
dE/dt = 0 → energy conserved
dL/dt = 0 → angular momentum conserved

Notation: Leibniz vs Newton

Leibniz (d/dt) Newton (dot) Meaning
dx/dt ẋ Velocity
d²x/dt² ẍ Acceleration
dp/dt ṗ Force

Leibniz notation makes the variable explicit; Newton's dot is compact for time derivatives.

12. Dimensional Analysis

Units as Constraint

Every physical quantity has dimensions (L, M, T, etc.). Equations must be dimensionally consistent—you cannot add meters to seconds.

The Seven SI Base Dimensions

Dimension Symbol SI Unit
Length L meter (m)
Mass M kilogram (kg)
Time T second (s)
Current I ampere (A)
Temperature Θ kelvin (K)
Amount N mole (mol)
Luminous intensity J candela (cd)

Dimensional Analysis in Action

Check Equation Validity

v² = u² + 2as
[L²T⁻²] = [L²T⁻²] + [LT⁻²][L]
✓ Consistent

Derive Relationships

The period of a pendulum T must depend on length L, mass m, and gravity g. Dimensional analysis gives:

T ~ √(L/g)
(Mass doesn't appear—surprising but correct!)

Convert Units

Energy: [E] = ML²T⁻²
1 joule = 1 kg·m²/s²

Buckingham π Theorem

If a physical problem involves n variables with k independent dimensions, then there are n − k dimensionless groups that govern the physics.

Dimensionless Numbers

Ratios of similar quantities are dimensionless and often control system behavior:

Number Definition Physical Meaning
Reynolds (Re) ρvL/μ Inertial/viscous forces (turbulence)
Mach (Ma) v/csound Speed relative to sound speed
Fine structure (α) e²/(4πε₀ℏc) EM coupling strength ≈ 1/137

13. Summary: Interconnections

The Unity of Physics

These themes are not independent—they interweave throughout physics, reinforcing and explaining each other.

Key Interconnections

1. Linearity ↔ Superposition ↔ Conservation

Linear systems allow superposition. Superposition enables conservation laws (e.g., momentum adds in collisions because F = ma is linear).

2. Symmetry → Conservation (Noether)

Every continuous symmetry implies a conservation law. This connects abstract geometry to tangible physics.

3. Feedback → Stability ↔ Linearity

Negative feedback creates stability by linearizing around equilibrium. Positive feedback drives systems away from linearity into chaos.

4. Local → Global

Local conservation laws (differential form) integrate to give global conservation (total quantities).

5. Quantization → Discreteness ↔ Integers ℤ

Quantum mechanics restricts continuous classical variables to discrete sets indexed by integers (n, l, m).

6. Rates of Change → Differential Equations

Nearly all physics laws are statements about derivatives. Solving them gives time evolution.

The Meta-Lesson

Physics is not a catalogue of facts but a coherent framework built on a small number of deep principles. When you understand these core themes, seemingly disparate topics—optics, thermodynamics, quantum mechanics—reveal themselves as different manifestations of the same underlying ideas.

Practical Value for IB Physics

  • Problem-solving: Recognize which principle applies (conservation? superposition? dimensionality?)
  • Conceptual understanding: See connections between topics (Why does Lenz's Law resemble Le Chatelier? Both are negative feedback!)
  • Exam success: IB examiners reward insight into underlying principles, not just plug-and-chug

From Memorization to Understanding

Instead of memorizing 100 disconnected formulas, understand 10 core themes and derive what you need. Physics becomes a language you speak, not a script you recite.

Final Thought

These themes cut across all of physics because they reflect the structure of reality itself. They are not arbitrary human inventions but patterns woven into the fabric of nature. When you master them, you're not just learning physics—you're learning to think like nature thinks.
